Peacekeeping Reform

United Nations peace operations have reached an identity crisis. Budgets shrink, missions end and principles are under strain. This page explores proposals to revitalise peacekeeping, safeguard core values and address emerging risks that threaten fragile peace processes.

Identity Crisis & Principles

High‑level discussions suggest UN peacekeeping is experiencing an identity crisis. Budgets have decreased, missions have ended and the Secretary‑General appears disengaged. Yet peacekeeping still offers an adaptable tool if anchored to its core principles of consent, impartiality and non‑use of force except in self‑defence【126879280082271†L165-L260】. Calls for reform emphasise championing principled peacekeeping through strategic communication, knowledge‑sharing between veterans and new personnel and innovation funding【126879280082271†L165-L260】.

How can peace operations remain relevant while confronting asymmetric threats and complex political environments? What reforms are needed to restore confidence among host nations and to align mandates with achievable political solutions?

New Agenda for Peace

The New Agenda for Peace advocates a political economy approach, universal prevention and support to regional actors while encouraging normative convergence. It calls for demilitarisation and emphasises the link between inequality and conflict【182919173124926†L150-L176】. Future peacekeeping could pivot from militarised interventions towards mediation, protection and capacity building.

What would a demilitarised peace operation look like? How can missions support host governments in addressing inequality and structural drivers of violence? Consider how local and regional actors can be empowered to lead sustainable peace efforts.

Guardrails for Emerging Risks

The agenda proposes guardrails to manage emerging risks such as climate change, biological threats, artificial intelligence and outer space activities. It advocates norms like prohibiting attacks on civilian infrastructure and developing legal instruments to ban lethal autonomous weapons【182919173124926†L266-L286】. A formula is offered: converge norms, create structures to verify compliance, establish accountability mechanisms and align financial flows for prevention and peace【182919173124926†L331-L346】.

How can such guardrails be implemented within peace operations? What mechanisms can ensure accountability across state and non‑state actors when new technologies blur traditional boundaries?

Future Generations & Digital Peace

Peace operations must account for the rights of future generations and the increasing role of technology. The 2024 Summit of the Future introduced a Global Digital Compact that seeks to close digital divides, ensure safe digital spaces and enhance AI governance【471743158865615†L324-L394】. Integrating foresight into mission planning could help anticipate digital threats and harness new tools for peacekeeping.

Imagine a peace mission equipped with AI‑assisted early warning, community‑led digital inclusion programmes and transparent data governance. What safeguards are necessary to prevent misuse and to uphold human agency? How might such innovations help sustain peace beyond the mission’s lifespan?
